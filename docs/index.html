Recursive Language Models

A new way to use LLMs on large documents

One sentence: Instead of feeding an entire document into an AI model at once, let the model break the work into smaller pieces — just like a person would.

No prior knowledge needed. We'll walk through the problem, the idea, and how it works step by step.

What you'll learn

By the end of this deck you'll understand what an RLM is, why it matters, and how it differs from the standard way of using AI models.

The problem

AI models can only read so much at once

Every model has a "context window" — a hard reading limit
What happens today
  • You paste a long document into ChatGPT or another AI model.
  • If the document is too long, it gets cut off or the model loses track of details buried in the middle.
  • The model has to do everything at once — find the answer AND read the whole document.
Why this is a problem
  • Important details get lost in long documents.
  • The model can't go back and re-read a specific section.
  • Bigger context windows help, but the quality still drops with length.

Analogy: Imagine being asked to summarize a 200-page report — but you can only glance at it once, for 30 seconds, and you can't take notes.

The insight

What would a human do instead?

Divide and conquer — an idea as old as problem-solving itself
1

Skim the document. Get a sense of how it's organized — headings, sections, length.

2

Break it into sections. Decide which parts are relevant and split the work up.

3

Work on each section. Read and answer questions about one piece at a time.

4

Combine the results. Merge all the answers into one final response.

Key idea: An RLM lets the AI model follow this same strategy — skim, split, work on pieces, and combine.

How it works

The model becomes a manager, not just a reader

Recursive = it can keep breaking problems down until they're easy
What makes this different
  • The document is not crammed into the prompt.
  • The model reads only the pieces it needs.
  • Sub-tasks can break down further if they're still too complex.
  • The final answer combines all the small results.

Side by side

Traditional LLM vs. RLM

Same question, different approach
Traditional approach 
# Stuff everything into one prompt
answer = model("Here is a 50-page report: ...
Summarize it.")

The model sees the entire document at once. Details get lost, the prompt may exceed the model's limit, and quality degrades with length.

RLM approach 
# Give the model a task + let it
# work through the document in pieces
answer = rlm("Summarize this report",
             fifty_page_report)

The model breaks the work down itself. Each piece gets focused attention, and results are combined into a final answer.

Why this matters

Better answers, not just longer inputs

It's not about reading more — it's about working smarter
Works at any scale
  • Handle documents far larger than any model's reading limit.
  • Each sub-task is small, so the model can focus and give better answers.
  • The same approach works for summaries, Q&A, analysis, and more.
More reliable
  • Important details don't get buried or missed.
  • The document is kept separate from the instructions, reducing confusion.
  • You can inspect what each step did — it's transparent, not a black box.

Takeaway: The win isn't just "more text." It's giving the model a structured, human-like way to work through complex tasks.
